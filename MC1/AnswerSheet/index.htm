2 ― Characterize any biases you identify in these news sources, with respect to their representation of specific people, places, and events. Give examples. Please limit your answer to 6 images and 500 words.

Bias Detection in News Reporting
Hypothesis: Certain news outlets demonstrate political or ideological bias by using emotionally charged language or selective reporting when discussing GAStech, APA, or the POK.

Approach: We ran VADER sentiment analysis, applied framing lexicons, and linked entity mentions to sentiment trends.

Key Takeaways:
• GAStech coverage is balanced and neutral.
• POK is highly polarized, with extreme positive and negative camps.
• APA receives consistently negative reporting, implying one-sided bias.

Findings
  • GAStech Coverage: Sentiment bars cluster tightly around zero (±0.2), indicating mostly neutral reporting. A few mildly positive (+0.4) and mildly negative (‒0.4) outliers suggest a balanced tone with low ideological push.
  • POK Coverage: Sentiment spans a very wide range (+0.9 to ‒1.0). Most outlets score strongly negative, with a handful of positive outliers, indicating high polarization in how the movement is portrayed.
  • APA Coverage: Uniformly negative across all outlets (scores between ‒0.6 and ‒0.9), despite fewer articles. This one‒sided portrayal implies pervasive derogatory framing.
  • Framing Examples:
    • GAStech: "environmental stewardship" vs. "industrial giant" ‒ largely factual, mixed tone.
    • POK: "activists" vs. "radicals" ‒ correlates with extreme sentiment scores.
    • APA: "advocate" vs. "lobbyist" ‒ outlets favor the negative "lobbyist" label almost exclusively.

Conclusion: Coverage of GAStech is comparatively balanced and neutral. In contrast, reporting on the POK is highly polarized, and coverage of APA is consistently negative, revealing strong ideological bias in those outlets.

Identification of Official and Unofficial Relationships

Hypothesis: There are unofficial ties between GAStech employees and members of the POK or APA, potentially signaling ideological alignment or covert collaboration.

Approach: We built an integrated graph by combining:

  • Official edges from HR records & resumes (employee ↔ GAStech)
  • Inferred ties via email‒header metadata and co‒mentions in news & historical documents
  • NER & co‒reference resolution to link people ↔ organizations

Findings

  1. GAStech Core Team: GAStech forms a dense hub of red edges (official employment) and interlocking grey edges (internal email traffic). This cohesive subgraph reflects the company's internal staff network.
  2. External Bridges: A minority of grey edges link individual employees outward to POK, APA, and Government nodes‒none of which appear as formal ties.
  3. Government Connections: Seven employees show grey links to "Government"‒suggesting informal channels (ex:, advisory roles, lobbying events).
  4. POK & APA Links: Five employees connect to POK and three to APA via indirect evidence (co‒mentions in reports, shared email threads).
  5. Key Individuals: Three nodes‒Ingrid Barranco, Orhan Strum, Sten Sanjorge Jr.‒are the only ones linked to all four organizations, marking them as prime candidates for further investigation.
Key Takeaways
  • Nearly all non‒HR connections are unofficial, inferred from metadata or co‒mentions.
  • Formal (red) edges are limited to employee ↔ GAStech, confirming no direct documented ties to POK, APA, or Government.
  • Grey edges reveal hidden communication channels that span advocacy groups and regulators.
  • A small set of employees acts as "bridges" into POK, APA, and Government, suggesting potential leverage points.
  • This layered network highlights how informal ties can outnumber and outlast formal relationships.

Conclusion: While official employment ties confine most connectivity within GAStech, the abundance of grey, unofficial edges reveals significant informal relationships with POK, APA, and Government entities. This pattern underscores potential covert collaboration channels worthy of deeper qualitative inquiry.
